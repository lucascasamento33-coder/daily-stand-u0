Nobel Economist Edmund Phelps Dies at 92
Edmund Phelps, the economist who fundamentally changed how the world thinks about inflation and unemployment, has died at 92. Phelps won the Nobel Prize in 2006 for challenging what had been gospel among economists—that countries had to accept higher inflation as the price for keeping unemployment low. His work showed that trade-off was an illusion in the long run, reshaping central bank policy for decades. The insight meant policymakers couldn't just juice the economy with inflation to create jobs without eventually paying a steep price. His ideas became the intellectual foundation for how the Federal Reserve and other central banks approach their dual mandate of stable prices and full employment. Phelps spent most of his career at Columbia University, where his research extended beyond macroeconomics to questions about economic justice and what makes work meaningful. His death marks the loss of one of the towering figures in postwar economic thought. The timing is particularly poignant as central banks worldwide continue wrestling with the very inflation-unemployment dynamics Phelps first decoded.
